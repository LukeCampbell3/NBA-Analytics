The identity you’re building

Mechanism-first NBA analytics that is decision-grade:
	1.	Explain value (mechanisms) instead of only measuring it (one-number leaderboards).
	2.	Model constraints (clamps) so counterfactuals don’t become fantasy.
	3.	Quantify uncertainty + disagreement so claims are graded, not asserted.
	4.	Support counterfactuals (“Team X fit”) with a traceable chain: why → how → where it breaks → how confident.

Below are the pillars in explicit form, plus the “airtight” plan to make it real and defensible.

⸻

Pillar 1 — Define “value” as a decomposition, not a number

What it is

Represent a player as a value vector across mechanisms:

V = [V_{\text{on-ball}}, V_{\text{off-ball}}, V_{\text{spacing}}, V_{\text{creation}}, V_{\text{finishing}}, V_{\text{rim}}, V_{\text{POA}}, V_{\text{switch}}, V_{\text{rebounding}}, V_{\text{foul-risk}}, \dots]

Then compute team-/context-specific rollups:

\text{OverallValue(team)} = \sum_i w_i(\text{team needs}, \text{playoff context}) \cdot V_i

Why it’s unique

Most public tools give you “impact” or “stat tables.”
You give a causal-ish explanation of value: which parts exist, which scale, which don’t.

⸻

Pillar 2 — Separate intrinsic skill from deployment and environment

What it is

Split observed production into:

P_{\text{observed}} = P_{\text{intrinsic}} + P_{\text{context}} + \epsilon
	•	P_{\text{intrinsic}}: what should travel across teams
	•	P_{\text{context}}: team help/hurt, schedule strength, scheme usage
	•	\epsilon: noise

Your “bubble” isn’t pretending full isolation is possible — it’s an accounting system for what is plausibly intrinsic vs contextual.

Why it’s unique

You output the decomposition and directionality (“suppressed by current system”, “boosted by teammates”), not just a blended result.

⸻

Pillar 3 — Counterfactuals must be constrained by clamps

What it is

Every “Team X” projection is a constrained optimization:

\max_{\theta} \Delta \text{Value}(\theta, \text{Team}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \theta \in \mathcal{C}
	•	\theta: role parameters (usage, minutes, shot mix, defensive assignment)
	•	\mathcal{C}: clamps (role budgets, scenario caps, defense portability, contract feasibility, etc.)

Outputs required for identity
	•	best achievable role
	•	which clamps fired (and how hard)
	•	what would need to change to unlock more

Why it’s unique

Public analytics usually does “what if usage ↑” without forcing feasibility.
You make feasibility part of the result.

⸻

Pillar 4 — Confidence governance is part of the product

What it is

No strong claim without a confidence trace:

C = f(\text{data quality}, \text{sample}, \text{extrapolation}, \text{model agreement}, \text{isolation}, \text{clamps})

Confidence itself is decomposed so it’s inspectable.

Why it’s unique

You’re building an evaluator that can say:
	•	“This is plausible but not validated (why).”
	•	“This is validated (why).”
	•	“This is internally consistent but likely wrong (impact prior disagreement).”

That’s how you get “less smoke.”

⸻

Pillar 5 — Handle uniqueness and comparables honestly (comp density)

What it is

Comparables are conditional. You explicitly measure “how comparable is this player?”

D(p)=\frac{1}{k}\sum_{q\in \text{TopK}(p)} s(p,q)

Low D(p) ⇒ “unique role, comps are weak anchors” ⇒ confidence penalty / narrative constraint.

Why it’s unique

You prevent forced comps and forced conclusions.

⸻

Pillar 6 — Add two reality anchors: impact priors + defense portability

A) Impact sanity-check (anti-drift anchor)

You compare your conclusions to an independent impact family prior. If disagreement is large:
	•	flag for review
	•	reduce confidence
	•	require justification (injury, role mismatch, data issues, etc.)

B) Defense + matchup portability (playoff survivability anchor)

Defense becomes:
	•	a role identity (what they defend)
	•	a portability score (will it survive targeting?)
	•	a scenario clamp (limits the counterfactual role/team)

Why this is identity-defining

This is the “adult supervision” layer. Your system becomes self-auditing and playoff-aware.

⸻

Pillar 7 — Contracts + aging curves turn it into valuation

What it is

You’re not just saying “good/bad,” you’re estimating decision value:

\text{SurplusValue} = \text{ProjectedValue} - \text{ContractCost}

And you adjust projected value through archetype aging curves:

V_{t+1} = V_t + \Delta V_{\text{context}} + \Delta V_{\text{age}}(\text{age}, \text{archetype})

Why it’s unique

This is how you become closer to a front office tool: value relative to constraints, time, and cost.

⸻

Pillar 8 — The output format must encode the identity

Your reports and JSON must force the system to show its work.

Every player output should always include:
	1.	Identity snapshot (role, archetype, comp density)
	2.	Value decomposition (visible + hidden + defense + priors)
	3.	Counterfactual (best team, clamps, projected deltas)
	4.	Decision layer (risks, confidence trace, feasibility, contract/age)

If your output doesn’t encode this, the system’s identity will blur.

⸻

The airtight approach: “iron it out” plan

Keep these steps explicit and treat them like a build checklist.

1) Create a glossary (definition contract)

Goal: every metric/component has a stable meaning.

For each component (examples you gave):
	•	spacing_gravity_skill
	•	suppression_relief
	•	portability_score
	•	impact_prior_disagreement
	•	comp_density
	•	clamp_severity

Define:
	•	what it is (plain language)
	•	input fields used (card dependencies)
	•	output scale (0–1? 0–100? units?)
	•	how to interpret high/low
	•	known failure modes (“this can be wrong if X is missing”)

This is what makes the system legible and maintainable.

2) Standardize outputs (one schema for every tool)

Goal: tools become composable, and you can backtest easily.

Do this with:
	•	a common header: player_id, season, team, generated_at, tool_version
	•	a common signals block (your vector components)
	•	a common confidence block (components + final)
	•	a common warnings block
	•	a common evidence block (which fields were missing, sample size proxies, comp density)

If outputs vary per tool, you can’t calibrate confidence reliably.

3) Backtest calibration (make confidence truthful)

Goal: “0.70 confidence” means ~70% hit rate for the defined event.

Step A: pick a season window

Example: 3–5 seasons; train/calibrate on earlier, validate on later.

Step B: define “breakout label” operationally

Pick labels that can be measured:
	•	Impact breakout: \Delta in impact prior (e.g., +X points/100)
	•	Role breakout: usage/minutes increase + efficiency maintained/improved
	•	Ecosystem breakout: impact prior rises without big box score rise (your key niche)

The “label definition” is crucial — it’s what turns the system from narrative to measurable.

Step C: calibrate thresholds so confidence matches hit rates

You’re doing probability calibration:
	•	bucket predictions by confidence (0.5–0.6, 0.6–0.7, etc.)
	•	compute empirical success in each bucket
	•	adjust weights/thresholds until the curve matches

This is what makes your confidence system not “made up.”

4) Measure drift (protect against internal-consistency hallucinations)

Goal: detect when your model is confidently wrong relative to reality anchors.

Track:
	•	how often you disagree with impact priors
	•	which archetypes disagree most
	•	whether disagreements are systematic (bias) or isolated (edge cases)

Create explicit drift flags:
	•	“impact prior contradicts claim”
	•	“defense portability contradicts role expansion”
	•	“comp density too low for strong claim”

This is how you keep the system stable as you add features.

⸻

Why this produces “less smoke”

Because the system stops being “a set of opinions” and becomes:
	•	defined terms (glossary)
	•	standard outputs (schema)
	•	measured truthfulness (calibrated confidence)
	•	self-audited (drift + priors + clamps)

That’s what makes it feel closer to engineering than commentary.
